# A Few Teacher Steps Go a Long Way: Cost-Efficient On-Policy Data Augmentation for Agent Post-Training 

Junze Ye 1,∗ Jiayi Cheng 2,∗ Miao Lu 1,∗ Michal Mankowski 2 Jose Blanchet 1

Mohsen Bayati 11Stanford University 2New York University 

Abstract 

For LLM agents, supervised fine-tuning is not only about teacher labels’ quality, but also about which interaction contexts those labels condition on. Pure behavioral cloning uses full teacher demonstrations, creating a mismatch between teacher-induced contexts seen in training and student-induced contexts encountered at test time. Recent work addresses this mismatch by querying a teacher at contexts reached by the student, often with increasingly elaborate filtering of the teacher’s continuations. We instead frame on-policy data construction as a budget-allocation problem: under matched supervision resources, should teacher output be spent on more start-to-finish demos, longer continuations, outcome filtering, or broader coverage of learner-induced contexts? We formalize this design space through the rollout policy, switch-time distribution, continuation horizon, filtering rules, and two complementary costs: teacher inference generated before filtering and teacher supervision retained for SFT. Across HotpotQA, ALFWorld, and Terminal-Bench-Dev, bounded unfiltered teacher continuations at learner-induced contexts improve over pure behavioral cloning at matched budgets. On HotpotQA and ALFWorld, where we run the full comparison, few-step continuations match or exceed success-filtered and critical-context-filtered alternatives. Our findings suggest that a few teacher steps, placed at learner-induced contexts, can be a more cost-efficient supervision allocation than longer or more heavily curated teacher completions. 

1 Introduction 

Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) trains a language-model agent by teacher forcing on labeled assistant turns [Williams and Zipser, 1989]. In agent post-training, those labels are often generated by a capable but expensive teacher; the simplest dataset is then a corpus of end-to-end teacher demonstrations. We call this instantiation pure behavioral cloning, or pure BC for short. It is easy to implement, but inherits a familiar weakness from imitation learning: the student is trained on input contexts induced by the teacher, while at test time it acts on contexts induced by its own earlier decisions. For multi-turn agents, an early imperfect action changes the subsequent observation stream, after which the student may see contexts that are poorly represented in the teacher-demonstration corpus. This motivates DAgger-style interactive imitation learning [Ross et al., 2011, Kelly et al., 2019], and recent LLM-agent work has adapted the idea by querying a teacher at states reached by the student [Choudhury and Sodhi, 2025, Lyu et al., 2025, Lauffer et al., 2025, Li et al., 2026]. Once one collects teacher supervision at learner-induced states, however, the central design question is not simply whether to use “on-policy” data. It is how to allocate a limited supervision budget. Additional teacher output could be spent on more end-to-end teacher demonstrations, longer teacher continuations after a student prefix, outcome-filtered continuations, or bounded corrections spread across more learner-induced states. These choices can all be described as adding supervised data,

but their costs are not comparable under a generic unit such as “one training sample.” We therefore propose to track two concrete cost measures: Ci, the total teacher inference spent before filtering, and Ctr , the teacher-generated supervision retained and trained on per SFT epoch. This separates protocols that are cheap to generate from those that are cheap to train on after filtering. A second comparability issue is that even the phrase “randomly sample a learner state” is ambiguous. For episodic LM agents, the relevant state is a learner-induced context generated by a causal hierarchy: first a task prompt is sampled, then a variable-length sequence of interaction turns unfolds, and within each turn the assistant tokens are generated autoregressively with strong serial dependence. Once rollout lengths vary, averaging over this process at the episode level or at the pooled-turn level no longer induces the same context distribution. We make these choices explicit through a unified data-construction framework specifying the rollout policy, switch-time distribution, continuation horizon, filtering rules, and induced context distribution. This framework’s goal is not to introduce a more complex algorithm, but to modularize design choices of on-policy data augmentation protocols. We then study a stylized one-round augmentation setting. First, train an initial behavioral-cloning checkpoint on a teacher-demonstration corpus DBC . Next, roll out this student on training tasks, sample contexts from the learner-induced trajectories, and ask the teacher to continue from those contexts. Finally, reinitialize from the base student and train on DBC ∪ D OP . We call this recipe BC+OP. Our main variant, OP-S HORT , keeps every sampled teacher continuation but caps it to a small number K of teacher turns. The continuation length K controls a breadth-depth tradeoff: a few teacher steps can show local recovery behavior after a student-induced prefix, but longer continuations spend more budget on teacher-descendant contexts rather than on covering additional learner-induced states. We compare OP-S HORT with P URE -BC, which spends the budget on additional full teacher demonstrations, and with filtered variants OP-S UCCESS and OP-C RITICAL , which keep successful teacher continuations and, in the latter case, focus queries on tasks the student fails. Across HotpotQA (question answering with search tool) [Yang et al., 2018], ALFWorld (text-based embodied planning) [Shridhar et al., 2021], and Terminal-Bench-Dev (command-line coding tasks) [OpenThoughts-Agent Team, 2025], bounded teacher continuations at learner-induced states are a consistently cost-effective form of SFT data augmentation. At matched supervision budgets, OP-SHORT improves over scaling P URE -BC. On HotpotQA, where we run the full comparison, unfiltered few-step continuations match or exceed the filtered alternatives depending on the cost axis. On ALFWorld and Terminal-Bench-Dev, the best continuation length is larger than in HotpotQA, but additional teacher turns still do not monotonically improve performance. On Terminal-Bench-Dev, OP-S HORT with a small fraction of the SFT corpus and no reinforcement-learning stage matches the OpenThoughts-Agent baseline that uses the full corpus followed by reinforcement learning. Our results suggest a practical rule for agent SFT under constrained expert supervision budgets: use pure teacher demonstrations to obtain a reasonable initial student, then allocate additional budget to bounded teacher continuations at learner-induced contexts before investing in full rollouts or heavier filtering. The right continuation length is domain-dependent, but across our experiments a few teacher steps often go a long way. Our contributions are: 1. We formulate SFT data construction for agent post-training as a budget-allocation problem, separating teacher-inference cost Ci from retained SFT training cost Ctr .2. We provide a formal framework for describing on-policy data augmentation that makes explicit the context-generation policy, switch-time distribution, continuation horizon, filtering rules, and how they affect the induced context distribution in variable-length rollouts. 3. We empirically compare bounded unfiltered teacher continuations, filtered continuations, critical-context filtering, and pure behavioral cloning across three agentic benchmarks, finding that a few on-policy teacher steps are often the most cost-effective use of additional teacher supervision.

2.3 The Budget Allocation Problem in Fine-Tuning Dataset Construction 

Separate from the sequential decision process of an LLM’s rollout, researchers face a different decision problem when post-training it for downstream applications: how to allocate a given fine-tuning resource budget to maximize final performance on the test set. In this paper, the teacher is an advanced LLM whose model size and inference cost are much larger than the student’s, but it can be instantiated as a human expert too. We quantify the budget constraint in two separate axes: 1. Ci: Total teacher inference cost , before custom data filtering 2. Ctr : Per-epoch training cost , i.e. GPU hours needed on the (filtered) SFT traces Both Ci and Ctr are measurable either in tokens or the number assistant turns generated by the teacher. For Ctr , the GPU hours needed by an SFT training run is roughly proportional to the number of accepted teacher tokens on an epoch-normalized basis. Whether the spending on Ci or Ctr dominates a research team’s fine-tuning budget concern, we suppose, is often idiosyncratic. For example, a research group with a dedicated allocation on a large GPU cluster faces near-zero marginal training cost, so they would be far more cost-sensitive to external API inference calls ( Ci) needed to generate teacher completions than to the size of the resulting training corpus ( Ctr ). So rather than assigning an ad-hoc weight, our experiments visualize budget scaling under each extreme point respectively. Beyond inference and training compute, task diversity in Qtrain , which we proxy by |Q train |, also shapes a student’s learning efficiency; recall from §2.1 that (μ, Ps, Po, r ) is often implemented as an empirical distribution over Qtrain . To avoid confounding effect from this factor, we generally control 

|Q train | when comparing among OP and pure BC variants, so differences along the cost axes isolate the effect of the data-construction recipe alone. Any variation in |Q train | is explicitly indicated in a plot. We treat |Q train | as a hyperparameter rather than a third budget axis since it is often much more expensive and inelastic than Ci and Ctr : the pool of domain-specific training tasks with verifiable answers is typically determined by whatever open-source or proprietary datasets are available at the fine-tuning phase’s onset, and meaningfully expanding its coverage requires highly costly human annotation effort that is qualitatively different from scaling teacher inference or GPU compute.

3 Experiments 

We test the cost-and-performance framing of §2 on three distinct agentic environments. On HotpotQA [Yang et al., 2018] (search-based question answering) we compare all four recipe types in Table 1, including varying K for OP-S HORT and n for P URE -BC. On ALFWorld [Shridhar et al., 2021] (embodied household planning in text environments) we compare OP-S HORT at K = 3 to pure BC at four training-instance budgets, with in-distribution and out-of-distribution evaluation, an ablation on the ratio of on-policy to expert-prefix data. On Terminal-Bench-Dev [OpenThoughts-Agent Team, 2025] (solving code problems in an interactive shell) we sweep pure BC across five fractions of the BC corpus and run OP-S HORT at two continuation budgets across three prefix policies, and compare with the OpenThoughts-Agent baseline. For the three OP types in all our experiments, ρ is π1 from Eq. 14, and the final augmented SFT set is DBC ∪ D OP (the same DBC used to obtain π1). 

3.1 Search Agent: HotpotQA 

HotpotQA [Yang et al., 2018] was originally designed to test a model’s ability to answer a question by synthesizing evidence from preset Wikipedia snippets. We use the Search-R1 + VeRL adaptation [Jin et al., 2025, Sheng et al., 2025], which removes the preset snippets and turns each question (i.e. task) into a sequential interaction with a searchable database: the agent interleaves thinking with search queries to a 2018 snapshot of the English Wikipedia, receives retrieved passages as observations, and terminates by submitting an answer in no more than Tmax = 5 turns. On-policy supervisions improve SFT sample efficiency. On both cost axes in Figure 2, teacher completions from π1-induced prefixes make better use of the fixed question set than additional PURE -BC demonstrations. PURE -BC improves as more complete teacher traces are added but enters a region of diminishing returns, whereas the OP recipes achieve higher pass@1 at comparable supervision budgets, suggesting that once naive scaling of expert demonstrations saturates, redirecting teacher supervision toward student-induced contexts offers a higher marginal return per token spent. 

Scaling teacher continuation length has non-monotone returns. The OP-S HORT K-sweep shows that longer teacher continuations are not automatically better, even before cost normalization. Increasing K helps up to K = 3 , but K = 5 is worse despite spending more teacher tokens. This non-monotonicity likely reflects two factors: the main learning is the bounded repair at the student-induced state, beyond which extra turns produce completions unlikely for a student; and in HotpotQA, trajectories that exhaust all Tmax might correlate with more ill-posed questions, biasing long continuations to be less instructional. 

Filtering trades teacher inference for fewer training tokens. The filtered recipes, OP-S UCCESS 

and OP-C RITICAL , can look attractive on retained training cost Ctr because rejected continuations are not trained on. They are less favorable on teacher-inference cost Ci, where rejected teacher tokens are still charged. The gap between the two panels therefore clarifies the tradeoff: filtering is most useful when the SFT training compute is scarce, not when teacher inference is the binding resource.

4 Discussion 

The main practical lesson is not that “on-policy” data should replace pure behavioral cloning. Rather, it is useful as targeted augmentation once BC has produced a student whose mistakes define informative contexts. In this view, the key knob is how much teacher-descendant trajectory to buy at each learner-induced context state. Our results suggest that bounded continuations often provide enough local recovery signal, while spending the same budget on longer completions, additional full demonstrations, or success filtering can reduce coverage of distinct student states. The continuation budget K should therefore be treated as a domain-dependent breadth–depth parameter, not a universal constant. Filtering should likewise be chosen according to the binding cost axis: it can reduce retained SFT tokens, but rejected continuations still consume teacher inference. 

Limitations. We study one-round augmentation on three verifiable agentic benchmarks, with the most complete variant comparison on HotpotQA and ALFWorld. The conclusions may change for long-horizon tasks where recovery requires extended planning, for settings without automatic reward verification, or under iterative data collection. We also do not provide a theory predicting the optimal

DAgger and on-policy SFT. Our work builds on interactive imitation learning, especially DAgger [Ross and Bagnell, 2010, Ross et al., 2011, Ross and Bagnell, 2014, Kelly et al., 2019], which reduces compounding error by collecting expert labels at learner-induced states. Caccia et al. [2024] show that, for LLM agents, better prediction on expert trajectories need not translate to better downstream performance once the student acts on its own induced contexts, and mention DAgger as a potential remedy. Lauffer et al. [2025] are the closest prior work to our intervention-style setup: they roll in with the student to a random switch step, let the expert continue to termination, mask the student prefix during training, and filter by verifiable success and student repetition. This motivates our filtered baselines, whereas our main OP-SHORT method keeps every sampled continuation but bounds it to a few teacher turns. Other variants use privileged train-time teacher state [Choudhury and Sodhi, 2025], teacher-identified first-error corrections [Lyu et al., 2025], self-reflected teacher-guided corrections [Chen et al., 2025a], or turn-level teacher-student mixing for long-horizon software agents [Li et al., 2026]. Related methods also address trajectory-level error accumulation through step-level comparison [Wang et al., 2025], teacher-free first-error revision [Yuan et al., 2025], or off-policy selection of critical trajectory segments [Chen et al., 2025b, Lan et al., 2025]. 

On-policy distillation. A related line studies on-policy distillation (OPD) for autoregressive lan-guage models, training on student-sampled sequences while matching teacher behavior via generalized knowledge distillation [Agarwal et al., 2024], reverse-KL objectives [Gu et al., 2024], context inter-nalization [Ye et al., 2026], privileged self-distillation [Zhao et al., 2026b], verbal-score supervision [Xiong et al., 2026], or black-box discriminator-based distillation [Ye et al., 2025]. Concurrently, Zhao et al. [2026a] decouple prefix source from KL direction, identifying student-prefix forward-KL as DAgger-style on-policy SFT and student-prefix reverse-KL as OPD. Our setting focuses on explicit teacher completions at executable, learner-visited agent states; this is natural for multi-turn tool-use, supports black-box teachers, and lets us measure the budget in generated and retained teacher tokens rather than in token-level distribution queries. 

Rejection-sampling SFT and the SFT-as-RL connection. A complementary line analyzes outcome-filtered SFT in its own right. Qin and Springenberg [2025] show that SFT on success-filtered trajectories optimizes a lower bound on the sparse-reward RL objective and propose an importance-weighted variant that tightens this bound. This perspective varies which generated trajectories are retained; we additionally vary the state distribution by querying the teacher at learner-induced prefix states. Accordingly, we treat filtered SFT as one data-construction choice within a broader budget-allocation problem, rather than only as a stand-alone alternative to RL. 

Positioning relative to prior work. Recent agent post-training recipes often bundle several choices: prefix policy, switch-time sampling, continuation length, masking, filtering, and sometimes a subse-quent RL stage. We instead make these choices explicit and compare them under matched supervision resources, separating teacher-inference cost from retained SFT training cost. This isolates the ques-tion central to our experiments: once a reasonable behavioral-cloning student is available, whether additional teacher budget is better spent on more start-to-end expert demonstrations, longer or filtered teacher continuations, or short unfiltered corrections spread over more learner-induced contexts.
